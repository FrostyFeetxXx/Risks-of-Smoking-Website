  • Risks and Long-term Impacts

There are a lot of chemicals in cigarettes, and these chemicals combine to form even more harmful compounds that severely impact your health. You inhale those compounds when you smoke, and the people around you breathe the second-hand smoke exhaled by you. There is also no safe level of second-hand smoke exposure. Second-hand smoke has also been shown to cause the same effects as the person who is smoking. Smoking can make it harder for a woman to become pregnant, in fact, it may even have effects that persist throughout the child's entire life, affecting her baby’s health before and after birth. Smoking increases risks for:

  • Preterm (early) delivery
  • Stillbirth (death of the baby before birth)
  • Low birth weight
  • Sudden infant death syndrome (known as SIDS or crib death)
  • Ectopic pregnancy

Given that the largest U.S. cigarette companies sold about 217 billion cigarettes to wholesalers and retailers nationwide in 2018, it’s no surprise that cigarette butts are the most frequently littered item in U.S. beaches and waterways and second most littered item globally. Cigarette butts are often disposed of in the environment, on streets, sidewalks, and other public areas, and may then be carried as runoff to drains and ultimately end up polluting rivers, beaches, and oceans. Because cigarette butts are primarily made of plastic filters that don’t biodegrade, the butts that aren’t eaten by wildlife simply pile up on shorelines or at the bottom of bodies of water.
