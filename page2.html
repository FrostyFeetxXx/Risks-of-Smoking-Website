The nicotine in any tobacco product is quickly absorbed into the person's bloodstream when a person starts smoking. Upon entering the blood, nicotine immediately stimulates the adrenal glands to release the hormone epinephrine (adrenaline). Epinephrine stimulates the central nervous system and increases blood pressure, breathing, and heart rate. Just like hard drugs such as heroin and cocaine, nicotine activates the brain’s reward circuits and also increases levels of the chemical messenger dopamine, which reinforces rewarding behaviors, leading to the human brain craving more. Studies suggest that other chemicals in tobacco smoke, such as acetaldehyde, may enhance nicotine’s effects on the brain.

More than 16 million Americans are living with a disease caused by smoking. For every person who dies due to smoking, at least 30 people live with a serious smoking-related illness. Smoking has a high chance of causing diseases and ailments such as cancer, heart disease, diabetes, and lung diseases such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), which includes emphysema and chronic bronchitis. Smoking also decreases the effectiveness of the body's immune response, leading to increased risk for tuberculosis, pneumonia and rheumatoid arthritis.
